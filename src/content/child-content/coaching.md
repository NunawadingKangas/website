Kangas coaches support the development of our players, help create a positive team environment, and model the standards expected across the club. Coaches must also complete the required screening, training, and conduct requirements before they can coach a team.

## Coach Requirements

The Child Wellbeing and Safety Act 2005 and Victorian child safe requirements place obligations on organisations that work with children and young people. To be successfully selected as a Kangas coach, you must complete the following requirements and provide the correct proof before coaching.

### Working with Children Check

Volunteer coaches must hold a current Working with Children Check. This is free for volunteers and can be started online through the [Victorian Working with Children Check application service](https://service.vic.gov.au/services/working-with-children). After completing the online application, follow the instructions for submitting the application at an Australia Post outlet.

If you already have a Working with Children Check and need to renew it, you can also use the same online service.

Police officers and coaches under 18 years of age are exempt from this requirement, but must provide proof of exemption. Teachers are also exempt, but must [notify Working with Children Check Victoria of the organisations they volunteer with](https://service.vic.gov.au/services/teachers-notifications/).

### Member Protection Declaration

Coaches must complete the Basketball Victoria Member Protection Declaration. The declaration needs to be signed in front of a qualified witness, similar to a statutory declaration. Suitable witnesses include a pharmacist, school principal, or police officer.

Please email [coaching@nunawadingkangas.com.au](mailto:coaching@nunawadingkangas.com.au) if you need the current declaration form.

### Child Protection Course

All coaches must complete the free online PBTR - Child Protection and Safeguarding course provided through Sport Integrity Australia and Play by the Rules. The course takes approximately one hour, can be completed in stages, and provides a certificate on completion.

[Access the child protection course](https://elearning.sportintegrity.gov.au/blocks/androgogic_catalogue/index.php?c1=Courses)

### Code of Conduct

Coaches must abide by the Codes of Conduct adopted by Nunawading Kangas and ensure that players and spectators associated with their team do likewise.

- [MEBA Code of Conduct](https://www.nunawadingbasketball.com.au/About/Code-of-Conduct)
- [Basketball Victoria Codes of Conduct](https://api.basketballvictoria.com.au/api/assets/byuip6j02lssg4ok)
- [Basketball Victoria Coach Hub](https://www.basketballvictoria.com.au/get-involved/coach/hub)

You cannot coach without providing the correct proof. This is Basketball Victoria policy and helps avoid confusion or embarrassment for volunteers, families, and the club.

### Value Player Development

Training should balance skill development, game understanding, and team structure. Even with older age groups, where sessions often include more game situations and systems, coaches should keep working to improve players as individuals.

### Create a Positive Environment

For many players, especially in younger age groups, a coach's influence is significant. Long after the results are forgotten, players will remember how they were treated and what kind of environment they were part of.

### Be Part of Your Players' Lives

Be aware of what is happening for players outside basketball. Building trust improves relationships and helps players feel supported. No one will care how much you know until they know how much you care.

### Teach Decision Making

Structure is useful, but young players also need to learn how to make decisions based on what the defence gives them. Instead of only teaching plays, teach players how to play.

Helpful decision-making areas include:

- Reading an on-ball screen
- Reading a dribble handoff
- Reading a basket cut or back cut
- Responding when the defence switches
- Responding when a player cannot catch the ball where the play expects
- Responding to double teams

### Set Clear Defensive Rules

On-ball screens are common in junior basketball, so players need to understand how the team will defend them. Your approach should match the skill, speed, size, and strengths of your team, as well as the opponent.

Common options include:

- Blitzing or trapping the ball carrier
- Icing the screen and forcing the ball toward the baseline
- Switching match-ups
- Showing and recovering

### Win the Extra Point Game

Small areas can add up across a game. Coaches should help teams value:

- Free throws, including drawing fouls and avoiding unnecessary fouls
- Offensive rebounds and limiting opponents to one shot
- Defending open three-point shots and forcing contested attempts
- Fast breaks, including controlled attacking transition and strong defensive transition

### Prepare for Pressure

Every team should have a press breaker. Strong half-court offence and defence can be undone quickly if players do not have structure for beating full-court or half-court pressure.

Baseline and end-line plays are also useful ways to create scoring opportunities without needing to call a timeout.

### Coach Players Fairly and Individually

Players do not all respond to feedback in the same way. Treat players fairly, but recognise that some need reassurance and confidence, while others may need a firmer push to improve effort, focus, or decision making.

## Basketball Victoria Domestic Coaches Clinics

Basketball Victoria runs clinics for domestic coaches throughout the year. These clinics cover skills, drills, and basic coaching philosophy, and often feature experienced coaches such as Kevin Goorjian, Paul Lankford, and Ross Wignell.

[Visit the Basketball Victoria coaching courses and accreditation website](https://www.basketballvictoria.com.au/get-involved/coach/coaching-courses-accreditation)
